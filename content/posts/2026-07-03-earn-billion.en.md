Near the end of a lecture at the Oxford Union, Paul Graham asked the audience a question. He said: a company is worth three million dollars today and grows ninety-three percent every month. What's it worth after nine and a half months? He asked everyone to take out a calculator. There was a silence, then numbers started coming from all directions: over a billion. One point five billion. The students froze for a moment, then someone laughed — the kind of laugh you give when math frightens you, not from joy, but from the sudden disappearance of a wall you were sure was solid. Paul offered no commentary. He just said: "Right. That's all it takes."

A few minutes before that, he had mentioned something a well-known American politician once said: you can't earn a billion dollars honestly — you have to step on people to get there. It sounds like a powerful claim, and intuitively it makes sense. Paul didn't argue against it. He just asked one question: what model is she using to calculate how wealth accumulates? If you assume wealth is linear — a little more each month, compounding slowly until you somehow reach a billion — then extreme wealth becomes almost impossible to explain without suspecting something dark underneath. But exponential growth is brutal and honest. It doesn't care whether you're a good person or a bad one. It only looks at one thing: whether what you've built makes people want to tell their friends.

Paul said the company growing at ninety-three percent per month is a real one. What they did was simple: they made something users wanted to recommend on their own. Friend tells friend, word spreads — that's where the growth rate comes from. You don't need to take advantage of anyone. What you need is a deeper capacity to feel what other people are going through. He said the founders who succeed aren't the most technical or the best at marketing — they're the ones who most genuinely understand what other people need. The best startup ideas usually aren't found on purpose; they come from running into a problem yourself, failing to solve it with anything that already exists, and deciding to build it. He said young founders have an advantage no one else has: their today is everyone else's tomorrow. The things that frustrate you at twenty are often the things that will frustrate everyone else in five years. You're not predicting a need — you're already living inside it.

Paul never said the politician was wrong. He just put the calculation in front of the room and let people see for themselves. When you don't understand exponential growth, watching someone go from ordinary to billionaire in a few years, the first thought that surfaces is almost always: something must be hidden — something that can't stand the light — because linear intuition has no room for that number. What Paul was saying is that the mechanism of wealth accumulation doesn't depend on exploitation. That company didn't climb on anyone's back. It just needed to build something worth telling a friend about. The people who can do that aren't usually the smartest or the most resourced. They're the ones who feel other people's situations most deeply. Empathy isn't a byproduct of wealth. It's the source.
