Steven Bartlett is not someone who believes things easily. He dropped out at eighteen, started a company at twenty, and took it public on the Nasdaq at twenty-seven. His podcast, Diary of a CEO, is the most listened-to podcast in the UK. He makes decisions based on data and pushes until the other person runs out of answers. He once said: "My brother and I were very good at math, very rational. Scientists tend to lean toward atheism. So when a top mathematician says he believes in God, I'm curious."

That's how he ended up sitting across from John Lennox. John is a professor of mathematics and the philosophy of science at Oxford, with over seventy peer-reviewed papers to his name. He asked every question you might have asked too.

---

## "Isn't This Circular Reasoning?"

People often use the Bible to prove God exists — but the Bible was written by God. Using something to prove itself doesn't hold up logically, like writing "Steven is a lizard" on a piece of paper and then using that same paper as evidence. John didn't sidestep the question, but his answer was unexpected. He said no worldview can be fully verified from the outside — including science itself. Science presupposes that reason is reliable and the universe is comprehensible. Those foundations can't be proven by science.

Then he offered a comparison: "If there's a red Ferrari parked outside, you could sit by the window and discuss it for a thousand years — its engine, its design, whether it's worth existing. But you can't know it from inside. You have to go out." He noted that the Greek root of "skeptic" means "one who observes from a distance." Keep your distance, and you can remain unconvinced forever — and never truly know anything.

---

## "Could You Be Wrong?"

John didn't say no. He said honestly that from an academic standpoint, anything could theoretically be wrong. But he said that's different from the certainty you build through lived experience: "You could ask me whether I might be wrong about my wife loving me. Theoretically, yes. But across fifty-eight years of marriage, every piece of evidence points in one direction. My certainty comes from a life accumulated, not from logical deduction." Faith works the same way — not waiting until everything is certain, but making a decision to trust based on enough evidence, then moving forward.

---

## "Is Being Good Enough?"

John didn't say "No, you'll go to hell." He started with something else: most people assume Christianity works like this — try to be a good person, hope God is merciful, and at the end of your life face a kind of cosmic weigh-in where your good deeds outweigh the bad. He said this understanding is completely backwards.

He offered an illustration. He met his wife in Cambridge. Imagine if he had walked up to her and said: "If you strictly follow the recipe on page 152 of the cookbook to make an apple cake every year for the next forty years, I'll accept you. Otherwise, go home." Anyone would laugh — but that's exactly how many people understand God. This isn't Christianity. It's the biggest misunderstanding of Christianity. The core of the Christian message is called grace: not what you do to earn acceptance, but being accepted first, and the relationship beginning from there.

The question "Is being good enough?" assumes people have the capacity to consistently be good. But if you honestly examine the past week of your life against the standard Jesus sets in the Sermon on the Mount — not just actions, but motivations, thoughts, how you treated others — most people will find their shortcomings run deeper than they imagined. Humans hurt each other, hurt themselves, often knowingly. The problem isn't willingness; it's capacity. Christianity faces this directly. It doesn't offer a scoring system — it offers a completely different starting point: not "be good enough to be accepted," but "be accepted first, then have the power to change."

---

## "What About That Child?"

If God is all-knowing and all-powerful, why does he allow a child in Africa to be born with parasites eating her eyes? What did she do wrong? John said: "If God doesn't exist, that child has absolutely no hope. Her suffering is real, her life is broken, and then she's gone — nothing remains. The universe has no response to her pain. But if God exists, and has the power to raise Jesus from the dead, that means another world is real — and that child will receive restoration there. Remove God, and you haven't removed the problem. You've removed the hope." In the face of suffering, Christianity doesn't offer a satisfying explanation. It offers a way through that doesn't end in despair.

---

None of these questions made John flinch. He looked each one in the eye and gave an answer you might disagree with — but couldn't call evasive. If you've carried any of these questions, this conversation is worth your time.
