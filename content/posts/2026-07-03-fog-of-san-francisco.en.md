Nearly ten books lie unfinished or unread on my Kindle: *Dark Matter*, *A Man Called Ove*, *The Midnight Library*, *Wool*, *The Lincoln Highway*, and a few others. Most of them are translated novels, lives built out of the Western world. Great stories, but reading them always leaves a slight distance, an easy way to fall out of the moment. The human relationships in them are rooted in a history and culture that isn't the one I grew up in.

Last week, browsing Sacramento Library's collection on Libby, the app that brings together America's public library catalogs, I stumbled on a Chinese title I wasn't expecting: *The Fog of San Francisco*. A few chapters in, I found that much of it was written as diary entries, recording the author's observations during several trips to the United States in the 1950s and 60s, often set against Taiwan's social attitudes, values, and views on life at the time. The book was first published in 1974, more than half a century ago. Both the America and the Taiwan it describes have long since become something else. And yet the more I read, the more curious I grew about the person who had written it.

The author, Xie Bingying (謝冰瑩), was born in 1906 in Yiyang, Hunan. It was a China where the Qing dynasty hadn't yet fallen, where Empress Dowager Cixi was still alive, five years before the Wuchang Uprising. She later joined the Northern Expedition and wrote *War Diary*, becoming China's first known female soldier writer. She studied in Japan, returned during the war against Japan to help with relief efforts, and in 1948 made her way to Taiwan, where she taught at National Taiwan Normal University for nearly two decades. Only in her later years did she settle in San Francisco, where, inside that fog, she published this book.

I keep thinking about the intellectuals of that generation, and how they made their choices inside a world shaped like that. What they faced was a decision whose weight is hard for anyone today to fully imagine. Which direction is this country going, and do I stay and go with it, or leave while I still can? Many who stayed were later denounced and persecuted during the Cultural Revolution. Some spent the rest of their lives in prisons or labor camps. Xie Bingying happened to leave before the red wheel had fully begun to turn. She must have watched, from a distance, where that wheel eventually took the country. What did she make of the road she never walked?

The book takes its title from one of its essays. Out of everything she wrote, she chose that one to name the whole collection. Was it random, or did she mean something by it? What she wrote about was fog, the weather of a foreign place. What was on her mind, she left for us to wonder.

Maybe some things only become clear once you've reached a certain age, a certain point in life, and even then, clarity doesn't mean you can say it out loud. She seems to have let the weather carry what she never finished saying, leaving it in the fog for whoever picked up the book afterward, to guess.
